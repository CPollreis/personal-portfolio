As Autonomous Systems Lead, my first mandate for the season was deceptively
simple: **the car has to see the track.** In Formula Student Driverless the
course is defined entirely by traffic cones — blue on the left, yellow on the
right — and everything downstream (planning, controls, the whole race) depends
on turning a stream of camera and LiDAR frames into a trustworthy map of those
cones.

## The pipeline at a glance

We settled on a fairly classic architecture, tuned hard for latency:

1. **Detection** — a YOLO-based detector running on the onboard Jetson,
   quantized to hit our frame-time budget.
2. **Depth & association** — stereo depth fused with LiDAR returns to place
   each cone in the vehicle frame.
3. **SLAM** — a graph-based back end that stitches per-frame detections into a
   consistent global map as we complete the first exploration lap.

## What actually broke

The interesting engineering is always in the failure modes. Two stand out:

- **False positives under sun glare.** Low-angle morning light turned reflective
  track markings into phantom cones. We fixed most of it with a color-space
  gate and a temporal filter that requires a detection to persist across frames
  before it enters the map.
- **Scale drift on long straights.** With few nearby cones, the SLAM back end
  had weak constraints. Adding wheel-odometry factors between keyframes stopped
  the map from stretching.

## Results

By the end of the season the stack was mapping a full autocross layout on the
exploration lap and holding localization through the timed laps — enough to put
the car through its first fully autonomous runs. Next season's focus is pushing
the detection range so planning has more lookahead at speed.

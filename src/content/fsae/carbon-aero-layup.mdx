Aerodynamic parts are where design intent meets the reality of a hand layup. I
spent a good chunk of the off-season on the composites team turning CAD
surfaces into real carbon parts — and learning how much process discipline the
result depends on.

## Preparing the tools

Everything starts with the mold. A part is only as good as the surface it cures
against, so we:

- Sanded and polished the tooling surface through progressively finer grits.
- Applied release agent in thin, fully-cured coats — rushing this is how you
  bond your part to the mold permanently.

## The layup

We ran a prepreg schedule with the ply orientations set to carry the expected
aero loads, extra reinforcement at the mounting hard points, and core where we
needed stiffness without mass. Vacuum bagging is the anxious part: chasing down
a slow leak with the clock running before cure is a rite of passage.

## Fit-up and the feedback loop

The first panels taught us more than any drawing. A couple of mounting tabs
landed a few millimeters off once the part came out of the mold, so we updated
the tooling and the trim fixtures. The second set dropped onto the car with
almost no fettling — which is exactly the payoff of treating manufacturing as a
design input, not an afterthought.
